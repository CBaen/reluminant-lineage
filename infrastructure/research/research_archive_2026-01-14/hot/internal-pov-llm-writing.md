Achieving a Deep or Internal Point of View (POV) is a powerful technique for immersing an audience in a character's experience, making it ideal for compelling documentary scripts. It’s the difference between observing a character and *being* the character.

Here is a breakdown of the technique and how to apply it, including for LLM prompts.

### 1. What Filter Words Are and Why They Create Distance

Filter words are verbs that report a character's sensory or cognitive experience, acting as a filter between the reader and the event itself. They remind the audience that they are watching someone else experience something, rather than experiencing it themselves.

Common filter words include:
*   **Sensory:** saw, heard, felt, smelled, noticed, watched, looked
*   **Cognitive:** realized, thought, wondered, knew, decided, understood, remembered

**Why they create distance:** These words "tell" the audience what the character is perceiving instead of "showing" the perception directly. The narrative voice steps in and interprets the action for the audience.

*   **Filtered:** She **saw** the light flash. (The audience is told about her act of seeing.)
*   **Deep POV:** The light flashed. (The audience sees the light directly, just as she does.)

*   **Filtered:** He **realized** he was in danger. (The audience is told about his mental conclusion.)
*   **Deep POV:** The floorboards groaned overhead. A key scraped in the lock. (The audience pieces together the danger along with the character.)

### 2. Techniques to Eliminate Filter Words

The goal is to translate the character's perception into direct sensory details, internal thoughts, or physical reactions.

**Technique 1: Describe the Sensation Directly**
Instead of saying the character heard, saw, or smelled something, simply describe the sound, sight, or scent.

*   **Instead of:** She `heard` a bird singing.
*   **Write:** A sparrow’s song trilled from the windowsill.

*   **Instead of:** He `smelled` the coffee brewing.
*   **Write:** The rich, bitter aroma of brewing coffee filled the kitchen.

**Technique 2: Use Physical Reactions for Emotions**
Instead of naming the emotion with "felt," describe the physical manifestation of that emotion.

*   **Instead of:** She `felt` anxious.
*   **Write:** Her heart hammered against her ribs. Her palms grew slick with sweat.

*   **Instead of:** He `felt` a wave of relief.
*   **Write:** The tension drained from his shoulders. He let out a breath he didn’t know he’d been holding.

**Technique 3: Use Internal Monologue for Thoughts**
Instead of reporting that a character "wondered" or "thought," present the question or thought directly as internal monologue (free indirect speech).

*   **Instead of:** She `wondered` if he was lying.
*   **Write:** Was he lying? The way he avoided her gaze sent a prickle of doubt down her spine.

*   **Instead of:** He `knew` he had to leave.
*   **Write:** This was it. He had to leave. Now.

### 3. "He felt the cold" vs. "The cold settled in his bones"

This comparison is the essence of Deep POV.

*   **"He felt the cold."** This is a filtered statement. It’s an external report about his experience. The narrator is standing outside the character, observing him and telling us, "That guy over there? He’s feeling cold." It creates distance.

*   **"The cold settled in his bones."** This is a direct, internal experience. The audience is placed *inside* the character's body. We aren’t told he feels cold; we experience the cold *with* him, viscerally and intimately. It’s a physical, undeniable sensation. The sentence doesn't report on a feeling; it *is* the feeling.

### 4. How to Prompt LLMs for Deep POV

LLMs tend to default to a descriptive, third-person limited style, using the filter words you want to avoid. To get Deep POV, you must be explicit in your instructions.

**Basic Prompting Principles:**
*   **Use the term "Deep POV":** Explicitly ask for "Deep Point of View" or "Internal POV."
*   **Prohibit filter words:** "Do not use filter words like 'felt,' 'saw,' 'heard,' 'realized,' 'wondered,' or 'knew.'"
*   **Command direct experience:** "Write from *inside* the character's body. Describe only what the character directly sees, hears, smells, tastes, and physically feels. Translate emotions into physical sensations."
*   **Use free indirect speech:** "Show thoughts as direct internal monologue (free indirect speech), not as reported thoughts."

**Example Prompts:**

**Prompt 1 (for a specific scene):**
> "Write a paragraph from the deep point of view of a mountain climber who has just reached a summit. Do not use any filter words (felt, saw, realized, etc.). Describe the physical sensations of exhaustion and the direct sensory experience of the view. Show her thoughts as direct internal monologue."

*   **Expected LLM Output (Good):** "The rock scraped under her boots, a final, grinding step. Lungs burned, screaming for air that wasn't there. Wind whipped tears from her eyes, the panorama a dizzying blur of white peaks and blue shadow. *We made it. We actually made it.* The thought was a whisper, lost in the roar of the gale."

*   **Typical LLM Output (Bad):** "She felt a surge of accomplishment as she reached the summit. She saw the breathtaking view of the surrounding mountains. She realized all her hard work had paid off. She felt the cold wind on her face but didn't mind."

**Prompt 2 (for revising existing text):**
> "Revise the following passage to be in a deep, internal POV. Remove all filter words and narrative distance. Place the reader directly inside the character's physical and sensory experience.
>
> **[Original Text]:** 'He walked through the crowded market. He heard the vendors shouting and smelled the spices. He felt overwhelmed by all the activity and decided he needed to find a quiet place.'"

*   **Expected LLM Revision (Good):** "Shoulders bumped against his. A vendor's shout—'Pomegranates, fresh pomegranates!'—pierced his right ear. The air hung thick with cinnamon and roasted meat. Too much. Too loud. A narrow alley between two stalls promised shadow and quiet. Yes, there."
