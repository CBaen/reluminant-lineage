# Sweep 4: Clarity & Polish

**Priorities:** P3 (Name Economy), P10 (Pronoun Clarity), P14 ("You" Density)

This sweep ensures audio clarity. Listeners cannot glance back—every sentence must be immediately comprehensible.

---

## P3: Name Economy

**Requirement:** Maximum 6 unusual names per episode. One-time characters get descriptions, not names.

### Why This Matters

Every unusual name costs:
- Production time (TTS testing, caption work)
- Listener cognitive load (memory)
- Implied importance ("Remember this person")

A name is a PROMISE. If the character doesn't return, the promise is broken.

### What Counts as "Unusual"

- Non-English names requiring pronunciation work
- Historical figures with titles
- Multiple names for same person

### What Doesn't Count

- Common first names (John, Mary)
- Well-known historical figures (Tesla, Edison)
- Generic titles (the doctor, the captain)

### Characters That EARN Names

| Criterion | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Testimony spanning multiple paragraphs | Key witness |
| Real historical figure | Adds documentary authority |
| Substantial arc within episode | Witness → react → changed |
| Emotional weight | If suffering is the point, name creates intimacy |

### How to Replace Unnecessary Names

**Before:**
> "Lieutenant James Whitford was on deck. Ensign Michael Torres reported to Whitford. Chief Petty Officer Sarah Chen documented the exchange."

**After:**
> "The lieutenant was on deck. A young ensign reported to him. The chief petty officer documented everything."

### Multi-Witness Episodes

Exception: When each perspective matters (e.g., Philadelphia Experiment through 4 sailors), names can exceed 6. But rare.

### Checklist

- [ ] Counted unusual names (target: ≤6)?
- [ ] One-time characters have descriptions, not names?
- [ ] Named characters appear multiple times or have emotional weight?
- [ ] No name-heavy introductions (3+ names in one paragraph)?

## P10: Pronoun Clarity

**Requirement:** Re-anchor character's name every 2-3 sentences. No pronoun avalanches.

### Why This Matters

Text readers can glance back. Audio listeners only move forward. Nine consecutive "she" sentences = listener loses track of WHO.

### Gold Standard Pattern

Name → Pronoun → Pronoun → Name → Pronoun → Name

**Example:**
> "She looked at the space. She saw a hole. Marta described it as geometric absence.
>
> Marta refused to hold him. She cut the cord. Marta told her sister about the vibration."

### Red Flags (Fix These)

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| 8+ consecutive pronoun sentences | "Pronoun avalanche" |
| Short staccato sentences with only pronouns | Creates lists, not narrative |
| Same pronoun in long paragraph without name | Listener grip loosens |
| Pronouns when character identity is unclear | Which "she"? |

### How to Fix

**Before (avalanche):**
> "She walked to the door. She hesitated. She turned the handle. She felt the cold metal. She pushed. She stepped through. She looked around. She saw nothing."

**After (anchored):**
> "She walked to the door. She hesitated. Marta turned the handle, feeling the cold metal bite into her palm. She pushed through and looked around. The room was empty."

### When Extended Pronouns Are Acceptable

- Internal monologue (listener fully identified with POV)
- Rapid action sequences (brief runs maintain momentum)
- Deep POV intimate moments

But always re-anchor at paragraph breaks.

### Checklist

- [ ] No more than 3 consecutive pronoun sentences?
- [ ] Names re-anchored at paragraph breaks?
- [ ] Listener always knows who "she/he" refers to?
- [ ] No confusion between multiple characters of same gender?

## P14: "You" Density

**Requirement:** Save "you" for the ending. Maximum 8 uses before Metastasis section. 80% should appear in Prognosis.

### Why This Matters

At the climax, "you" should feel like a finger pressing into the listener's chest. If "you" appears 50 times already, the word has no weight. Scarcity = power.

### Gold Standard (Episode 2 Ending)

> "Tonight, when the sun goes down, look at the twilight. Watch the colors bleeding out of the sky—the oranges, the purples, the deep reds that the old light used to cast. And ask yourself. Before today, before you heard any of this—what color did you think Chartreuse was? If you saw red, you are carrying something... Hold onto it."

The "you" lands because it was EARNED through scarcity.

### Acceptable "You" in Documentary Sections

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| Opening hook question | "Have you ever heard a dead machine scream?" |
| Interactive moment | "Close your eyes. Visualize the logo. Can you see the basket?" |
| Rare positioning | "Consider the Cornucopia" (intentional invitation) |

### Parasitic Patterns (Flag and Fix)

These drain "you" of power:

| Pattern | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| "If you go to..." | "The archive contains..." |
| "If you look at..." | "The records show..." |
| "You know the..." | Remove entirely |
| "You see..." | Remove entirely |
| "You can imagine..." | Remove entirely |
| "You might wonder..." | "The question is..." |

### How to Count

1. Search for all "you" and "your"
2. Count instances before Metastasis section
3. Target: ≤8 before Metastasis
4. Prognosis section: high density is EARNED

### Checklist

- [ ] Counted "you/your" (target: ≤8 before Metastasis)?
- [ ] Removed parasitic "you" patterns?
- [ ] "You" concentration in Prognosis ending?
- [ ] Opening hook "you" (if used) creates intimacy?
- [ ] Final "you" feels like finger pressing into chest?

---

## Overall Clarity Check

After completing this sweep, the episode should have:

1. **Name economy** - ≤6 unusual names, one-timers described
2. **Pronoun clarity** - Always know who "she/he" is
3. **"You" power preserved** - Scarcity creates impact
4. **Audio comprehensible** - Listener never lost or confused
5. **Ending lands** - "You" in Prognosis feels direct and intimate
