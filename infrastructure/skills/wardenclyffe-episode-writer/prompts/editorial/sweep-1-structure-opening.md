# Sweep 1: Structure & Opening

**Priorities:** P11 (Opening Hook), P9 (Major Transitions), P12 (Setup Without Payoff)

This is the first sweep. It establishes whether the episode will hook listeners and keep them oriented.

---

## P11: Opening Hook

**Requirement:** First sentence must create vertigo or wrongness. NO dates, locations, or context.

### Why This Matters

Audio listeners decide whether to stay within SECONDS. The first sentence is everything.

### Gold Standard (Episode 2)
> "Have you ever heard a dead machine scream?"

- Direct question creates intimacy
- "Dead machine" is a paradox
- "Scream" implies suffering
- No dates, locations, or context
- Creates vertigo immediately

### Red Flags (Fix These)

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| "In eighteen fifty-six..." | Date first = history documentary = attention relaxes |
| "In a village called Smiljan..." | Location first = setting the scene = no hook |
| "To understand what happened..." | Lecture framing = listener disengages |
| "The document is held in an archive..." | Archival setup = no urgency |
| "The following is an investigation..." | Mission statement = not a hook |

### What Creates Vertigo/Wrongness

- Violation of expectation
- Death/violation of dignity
- Paradox or impossibility
- Direct address + uncanniness

### Checklist

- [ ] First sentence creates vertigo or wrongness?
- [ ] NO dates in first sentence?
- [ ] NO locations in first sentence?
- [ ] NO context/setup in first sentence?
- [ ] Listener would lean in, not settle back?

## P9: Major Transitions (Breath Paragraphs)

**Requirement:** Every location/time jump needs a breath paragraph that resets the listener's mental stage.

### Why This Matters

Audio is linear. Listeners cannot flip back to reorient. Without breath paragraphs, they spend 30 seconds figuring out WHERE they are instead of following the story.

### Gold Standard (Episode 2)

**Location change: Boston → Caribbean Sea**

Signal the shift:
> "The phenomenon was not confined to one city. Not confined to one wire."

Full breath paragraph:
> "Four thousand miles away. The Caribbean Sea. The brigantine Southern Cross. Captain Whitmore was in his cabin. It was noon. The ship was becalmed. The sails were hanging limp."

### Required Elements

Every major transition MUST include:
1. Signal the shift (close previous section)
2. Name the location explicitly
3. Ground in date/time
4. Introduce the focus (who/what we're following)
5. Sensory grounding (2-3 details making it real)

### Red Flags (Fix These)

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| No time established | Listener is temporally lost |
| No specific location | Listener is spatially lost |
| Abrupt "Meanwhile in..." | Feels jarring, cheap |
| "Let's go to..." | Narrator gets physical presence (P2 violation) |
| Topic labels as headings | "The Philadelphia Experiment" is not orientation |

### Small Transitions (Under 200 words)

Don't need full breath paragraph. Sentence reorientation is sufficient.

### Returning to Established Locations

Don't re-describe extensively. DO signal the return:
> "Back at the Hotel New Yorker, the agents were still cataloguing."

### Checklist

- [ ] Every location jump has breath paragraph?
- [ ] Every time jump has temporal grounding?
- [ ] Listener always knows where/when they are?
- [ ] No "Let's go to..." constructions?

## P12: Setup Without Payoff

**Requirement:** Everything introduced must either pay off within the episode or be explicitly marked as open thread for future episodes.

### Why This Matters

Listener's brain stores "this will matter." If payoff never comes, the slot stays open—listener feels absence. Setup without payoff trains listeners NOT to pay attention.

### The Test

For each setup/detail introduced:
1. Does this pay off within the episode?
2. If not, is it explicitly marked as open thread? ("The answer would not come for another decade.")
3. Can this section be removed without damaging the ending?

If YES to #3 → either add payoff or cut the setup.

### Types of Payoffs

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Direct | Question answered |
| Thematic | Detail explains character behavior |
| Implicit | Implication IS the resolution |
| Marked open | Explicitly noted for future episodes |

### Red Flags (Fix These)

- Folklore introduced but never connected to mystery
- Character mentioned but abandoned
- Concept explained but never used
- 500+ words of background with no connection to mystery
- Mysterious detail (locked trunks) introduced without revelation

### Series-Level Threads

Must be explicit:
> "The answer to that question would not come for another decade. We will return to this in Episode 12."

Should be rare. Overuse trains listener to ignore details.

### Checklist

- [ ] Every setup has a payoff?
- [ ] Or explicitly marked for future?
- [ ] No orphaned characters?
- [ ] No unexplained mysteries that feel forgotten?
- [ ] Background sections connect to main narrative?

---

## Overall Structure Check

After completing this sweep, the episode should have:

1. **Hook** - First sentence creates vertigo
2. **Orientation** - Listener knows where/when after hook
3. **Clear transitions** - Every jump is grounded
4. **Payoffs** - Every setup resolves or is marked
5. **Forward momentum** - Listener never confused about location/time
