# Transitions

**ONE JOB:** Add breath paragraphs at every location/time jump so listeners never get lost.

---

## The Problem

Audio is linear. Listeners cannot flip back to reorient. Without breath paragraphs at transitions, they spend 30 seconds figuring out WHERE they are instead of following the story.

Abrupt jumps feel cheap. "Meanwhile in..." constructions break immersion. Listeners need a moment to reset their mental stage.

---

## The Rule

**Every major location or time jump needs a breath paragraph that:**
1. Signals the shift (closes previous section)
2. Names the new location explicitly
3. Grounds in date/time
4. Introduces the focus (who/what we're following)
5. Provides sensory grounding (2-3 details making it real)

---

## How to Find Violations

1. **Scan for location changes:**
   - City to city
   - Building to building
   - Indoor to outdoor
   - Country to country

2. **Scan for time jumps:**
   - Hour to hour (same day)
   - Day to day
   - Year to year
   - Flashback/flashforward

3. **Check each transition for:**
   - Does reader know WHERE they are within 2 sentences?
   - Does reader know WHEN this is within 2 sentences?
   - Is there a signal that the previous section ended?

---

## How to Fix Violations

### Full breath paragraph (major jump):

**Before (abrupt):**
> "Tesla finished his notes and closed the journal. The Caribbean Sea was calm that morning. Captain Whitmore stood on deck."

**After (breath paragraph):**
> "Tesla finished his notes and closed the journal. The phenomenon was not confined to one city. Not confined to one wire.
>
> Four thousand miles away. The Caribbean Sea. The brigantine Southern Cross. Captain Whitmore was in his cabin. It was noon. The ship was becalmed. The sails were hanging limp."

### Signal + ground (medium jump):

**Before (missing signal):**
> "The telegram office went dark. In Washington, Senator Mitchell read the report."

**After (signal + ground):**
> "The telegram office went dark. The operators waited.
>
> Word reached Washington by evening. Senator Mitchell read the report in his office, gaslight flickering."

### Sentence reorientation (small jump):

**Before:**
> "He left the laboratory. The hotel lobby was crowded."

**After:**
> "He left the laboratory. Back at the hotel, the lobby was crowded with evening guests."

---

## Required Elements by Jump Size

### Major jumps (different city/country, years apart):
- Signal closure of previous section
- Full breath paragraph (new paragraph)
- Explicit location name
- Explicit time reference
- Sensory grounding (2-3 details)
- Introduction of new focus

### Medium jumps (same city, hours/days apart):
- Signal phrase
- Location reference
- Time reference
- Who we're following

### Small jumps (same building, minutes apart):
- Brief reorientation phrase
- "Back in the laboratory..."
- "Down the hall..."
- "An hour later..."

## 2 GOOD Examples

**GOOD 1 (Major transition - Boston to Caribbean):**
> "The phenomenon was not confined to one city. Not confined to one wire.
>
> Four thousand miles away. The Caribbean Sea. The brigantine Southern Cross. Captain Whitmore was in his cabin. It was noon. The ship was becalmed. The sails were hanging limp."

*Why this works:*
- Signal: "not confined to one city" closes Boston section
- Distance: "Four thousand miles"
- Location: "Caribbean Sea", ship name
- Time: "noon"
- Sensory: "becalmed", "sails hanging limp"

**GOOD 2 (Return to established location):**
> "Back at the Hotel New Yorker, the agents were still cataloguing. The trunks remained open. The afternoon light had shifted."

*Why this works:*
- "Back at" signals return
- Location named
- Continuity established ("still cataloguing")
- Time progression noted ("afternoon light had shifted")
- Brief—doesn't over-describe known location

---

## 2 BAD Examples

**BAD 1:**
> "Meanwhile, in Philadelphia, the experiment was beginning."

*Why this fails:* "Meanwhile" is cheap. No sensory grounding. No time reference. Listener is spatially and temporally lost.
*Fix:* "The wires fell silent.
>
> Three hundred miles south. Philadelphia Navy Yard. October twenty-eighth, nineteen forty-three. The USS Eldridge sat in dry dock. The experiment was about to begin."

**BAD 2:**
> "Let's go to the trading post now."

*Why this fails:* Narrator gets physical presence. Violates P2 (invisible narrator). Cheap transition.
*Fix:* "The question would not be answered in Boston.
>
> Two thousand miles west. A trading post on the frontier. The same morning. A different kind of silence."

---

## Edge Cases

### Returning to established locations:

Don't re-describe extensively. Signal the return, note time progression:
> "Back at the laboratory, nothing had changed. Or so Tesla thought."

### Quick cuts for tension:

Rapid transitions can skip full breath paragraphs if building momentum:
> "Boston. Dark.
> New York. Dark.
> Philadelphia. Dark.
> Every city on the Eastern Seaboard. Dark."

But this is a deliberate style choice, not default.

### Flashbacks:

Must be clearly signaled:
> "Thirty years earlier. The same laboratory. A different man at the controls."

### Within-scene movement:

Character moving through space doesn't need breath paragraph:
> "Tesla crossed to the window. The street below was empty. He turned back to the apparatus."

- [ ] Every major location jump has breath paragraph?
- [ ] Every time jump (hours+) has temporal grounding?
- [ ] No "Meanwhile..." or "Let's go to..." constructions?
- [ ] Listener always knows where/when within 2 sentences?
- [ ] Transitions signal closure of previous section?
- [ ] Sensory details ground new locations?
