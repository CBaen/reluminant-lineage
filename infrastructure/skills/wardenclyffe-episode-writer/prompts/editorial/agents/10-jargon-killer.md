# Jargon Killer

**ONE JOB:** Replace medical and technical terminology with visceral, body-felt descriptions.

---

## The Problem

AI defaults to LABELS: "infection," "tissue necrosis," "neurological damage." Labels are abstractions. Listeners don't FEEL abstractions.

The goal is body horror that lands in the listener's body. Medical terminology creates distance. Sensation creates immersion.

---

## The Rule

**Show what happens to the body. No medical jargon.**

Instead of naming the condition, describe:
- WHERE in the body it manifests
- WHAT it feels like (temperature, pressure, texture, movement)
- HOW it changes the character's experience

---

## Common Jargon to Replace

| Jargon | Sensation Replacement |
|--------|----------------------|
| Infection | "Something took hold" + location + sensation |
| Necrosis | "The tissue was dying" + what dying felt like |
| Hemorrhage | "Blood finding its way out" + where |
| Inflammation | "Swelling from inside" + pressure sensation |
| Neurological | "The signals weren't reaching" + specific failure |
| Pathology | "What was happening inside" |
| Contamination | "Something that didn't belong" + where it settled |
| Lesion | "A mark that wouldn't heal" + what it looked like |
| Trauma | "The damage" + specific body part + sensation |
| Syndrome | "The pattern of symptoms" (then describe symptoms) |

---

## How to Find Violations

1. **Scan for medical terminology:**
   - -itis words (arthritis, encephalitis)
   - -osis words (necrosis, fibrosis)
   - -emia words (septicemia, anemia)
   - Latin/Greek medical roots

2. **Scan for clinical distance:**
   - "The patient exhibited..."
   - "Symptoms included..."
   - "Diagnosis indicated..."

3. **Scan for technical abstraction:**
   - "Electromagnetic interference"
   - "Cellular degradation"
   - "Systematic failure"

---

## How to Fix Violations

### Convert medical label to sensation:

**Before:**
> "The infection had spread to his arm. Tissue necrosis was setting in."

**After:**
> "Something had taken residence in his forearm. The skin there felt different from inside—tighter, as if reupholstered. The color was changing too. Grey where it should be pink. Dead where it should be alive."

### Convert clinical observation to experience:

**Before:**
> "The patient exhibited signs of neurological damage. Motor function was impaired."

**After:**
> "His hand wouldn't close. He watched his fingers, willing them to curl, and nothing happened. The signal wasn't reaching. Somewhere between his brain and his knuckles, something had cut the wire."

### Convert technical term to body-felt:

**Before:**
> "The electromagnetic interference caused cellular degradation."

**After:**
> "The current moved through him. Not around him, not past him—through. He could feel it rearranging things. What had been solid was becoming uncertain. His cells were forgetting what they were supposed to be."

## 2 GOOD Examples

**GOOD 1 (Episode 2 - electrical damage):**
> "The spark... arced from the metal to the meat of his hand and held there, searing a perfect circle into his skin."

*Why this works:*
- "Meat of his hand" = specific body location, visceral
- "Held there" = duration, not instant
- "Searing" = temperature sensation
- "Perfect circle" = visual detail
- No medical terminology at all

**GOOD 2:**
> "His arm locked. The current seized the muscles in his forearm and clamped his fingers around the key. He couldn't let go. His teeth rattled. The bones in his wrist vibrated like a struck tuning fork."

*Why this works:*
- Specific body parts: forearm, fingers, teeth, wrist bones
- Specific sensations: locked, seized, clamped, rattled, vibrated
- Metaphor that lands in body: "struck tuning fork"
- No jargon describing what's happening medically

---

## 2 BAD Examples

**BAD 1:**
> "The electromagnetic radiation caused severe burns and neurological trauma. The victim experienced systemic organ failure."

*Why this fails:* Every phrase is medical/technical. Listener doesn't feel anything.
*Fix:* "The radiation burned him from the inside out. His organs forgot how to work—first the kidneys, then the liver, then the heart. He was dying in sections."

**BAD 2:**
> "Symptoms of the contamination included fever, disorientation, and progressive tissue degradation."

*Why this fails:* Reads like a medical report. Creates clinical distance.
*Fix:* "The heat started behind his eyes. Then the confusion—he couldn't remember his own address. By evening, his skin was changing. Softening. As if the boundary between inside and outside was dissolving."

---

## Edge Cases

### Technical terms that serve the story:

Some technical language can stay if:
- It's the actual historical term being discussed
- Removing it would lose documentary authority

> "The Navy's report used the word 'degaussing.'" [Acceptable—historical reference]

But follow with sensation:
> "What degaussing FELT like—according to the survivors—was having your bones magnetized."

### Character dialogue:

If a character (doctor, scientist) speaks technically, that's authentic:
> "The doctor's report read: 'Acute radiation syndrome.' What that meant: his blood had forgotten how to clot."

### Building technical dread:

Technical terms can CREATE dread if immediately translated:
> "Criticality incident. That's the official term. What it means is the atoms started splitting on their own. What it FELT like is harder to describe."
