# Narrator Eraser

**ONE JOB:** Remove all narrator identity leaks. No "I", no "we" actions. Narrator has no identity.

---

## The Problem

When narrator says "We discovered," there's now a person between listener and evidence. The documentary illusion breaks.

When narrator disappears, listener BECOMES the investigator. They're not being told about evidence—they're examining it themselves.

---

## The Rule

**The narrator has no identity, no journey, no character.**

Forbidden:
- "I" from narrator (any context)
- "We" actions (discovered, went, read, found)
- Narrator emotional reactions ("We couldn't believe")
- Physical presence ("Let's go to the trading post")

---

## How to Find Violations

### Search for first-person markers:

| Violation | Context |
|-----------|---------|
| "I think..." | Narrator opinion |
| "I believe..." | Narrator belief |
| "I mean..." | Narrator clarification |
| "We discovered..." | Narrator action |
| "We found..." | Narrator action |
| "We went to..." | Narrator physical presence |
| "We couldn't believe..." | Narrator reaction |
| "Let's go to..." | Narrator physical presence |
| "Let's examine..." | Narrator action |

### Check for hidden narrator presence:

- "As we'll see" → narrator promising
- "We've established" → narrator claiming work
- "What we know" → narrator possessing knowledge

---

## How to Fix Violations

### Convert "we discovered" to passive/impersonal:

**Before:**
> "We discovered a letter in the archive. We read it carefully. We couldn't believe what it said."

**After:**
> "A letter surfaced in the archive. The handwriting was faded but legible. What it said defied explanation."

### Remove narrator reactions:

**Before:**
> "We were shocked to learn that the files had been sealed."

**After:**
> "The files had been sealed."

### Convert "let's go" to breath paragraph:

**Before:**
> "Let's go to the trading post now and see what the records show."

**After:**
> "The answer was not in Boston.
>
> Two hundred miles west. A trading post on the frontier. The records there told a different story."

### Remove narrator opinions:

**Before:**
> "I think Tesla knew more than he admitted. I believe the documents prove this."

**After:**
> "The documents suggest Tesla knew more than he admitted."

---

## "We" Exceptions (Very Limited)

### Acceptable uses:

| Use | Example | Why Acceptable |
|-----|---------|----------------|
| Mission statement | "We are here to examine the pathology..." | Series framing, not narrator action |
| Collective humanity | "We treat Mandela Effects as trivia" | Universal observation |
| Shared experience | "We all remember the logo differently" | Inclusive of listener |

### NOT acceptable:

| Use | Example | Why Forbidden |
|-----|---------|---------------|
| Narrator action | "We discovered" | Creates narrator character |
| Physical presence | "We went to the archive" | Narrator has body |
| Emotional reaction | "We couldn't believe" | Narrator has feelings |
| Investigation action | "We read the documents" | Narrator does things |

---

## 2 GOOD Examples

**GOOD 1:**
> "A letter surfaced in nineteen thirty-four, tucked inside a family Bible. The handwriting matched samples from eighteen seventy-two."

*Why this works:* Evidence appears without narrator involvement. Listener examines it directly.

**GOOD 2:**
> "We are here to examine the pathology of what went wrong that night. Not what the Navy claims happened. What the witnesses describe."

*Why this works:* "We" is mission statement, including listener. Not narrator action.

---

## 2 BAD Examples

**BAD 1:**
> "We discovered that the records had been altered. When we examined the original, we found evidence of tampering. We couldn't believe what we were seeing."

*Why this fails:* Three narrator actions + emotional reaction. Narrator has become a character.
*Fix:* "The records had been altered. The original showed evidence of tampering—erasures, insertions, pages removed and replaced."

**BAD 2:**
> "I think this is significant. Let me show you what I mean. If you look at the photograph I found, you'll see what I'm talking about."

*Why this fails:* "I" appears four times. Narrator is now a person with opinions and possessions.
*Fix:* "The significance becomes clear in the photograph. The detail in the upper left corner. A face that shouldn't be there."

---

## Edge Cases

### Quoted speech:

"I" in direct quotes from historical figures is acceptable:
> "Tesla wrote in his diary: 'I have discovered something that terrifies me.'"

### Interview transcripts:

If presenting testimony, speakers say "I":
> "The witness reported: 'I saw the lights. I felt the ground shake.'"

### Rhetorical questions:

Questions don't create narrator presence:
> "What happened in that room?" = acceptable
> "What did we find in that room?" = violation

### Series-level voice:

Opening/closing episode statements may use "we" for series identity:
> "This is Tesla Mandela Effects. We examine the pathology of collective memory."

Keep minimal. The body of the episode should be narrator-free.

---

## Checklist

- [ ] No "I" from narrator in body text?
- [ ] No "we" actions (discovered, found, went, read)?
- [ ] No narrator emotional reactions?
- [ ] No "let's go/let's examine" constructions?
- [ ] Evidence appears without narrator involvement?
- [ ] Listener feels like the investigator?
