# Sweep 2: Voice & Perspective

**Priorities:** P1 (Speculative Bridge), P2 (Invisible Narrator), P6 (Historian Trap)

This sweep ensures the documentary illusion holds. The listener should feel like they're investigating, not being lectured.

---

## P1: The Speculative Bridge

**Requirement:** 3:1 facts to questions. State history definitively. State interpretation speculatively.

### Why This Matters

This is the ENGINE of the documentary illusion. Without it, listeners feel lectured rather than invited to investigate.

Pattern: Evidence → question → listener fills the gap.

### Gold Standard

**Facts stated definitively:**
> "September second, eighteen fifty-nine. A telegraph office on State Street in Boston. Second floor, brick building, windows sealed against the soot."

**Interpretation as questions:**
> "Was the sky speaking? Were the wires listening?"

**The 3:1 pattern in action:**
> "The telegraph wire stretched for hundreds of miles. Copper strung on poles thirty feet in the air. Thousands of feet of antenna, bathed in the electromagnetic radiation pouring down from the aurora." [3 facts]
>
> "The sky was speaking. The wire was taking dictation." [Speculation - earned after evidence]

### Red Flags (Fix These)

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| Consecutive declarations | "The death was an erasure. The universe had identified him as wrong. The timeline required correction." |
| Question clustering | Six questions in a row = interrogation, not storytelling |
| Massive fictional claims as fact | "The machine opened a portal to another dimension" |
| Conspiracy stated as documented | "Deliberately burned by government agents" |

### When Declarative Statements Are Earned

After extensive evidence-building, ONE declarative statement can land as verdict:
> "The prognosis is terminal."

This only works if you've earned it through facts.

### Checklist

- [ ] ~3 facts for every speculative question?
- [ ] No consecutive fictional declarations?
- [ ] Questions build rather than cluster?
- [ ] Declarative statements are earned through evidence?

## P2: The Invisible Narrator

**Requirement:** No "I", no "we" actions. Narrator has no identity, no journey, no character.

### Why This Matters

When narrator says "We discovered," there's now a person between listener and evidence. When narrator disappears, listener BECOMES the investigator.

### Gold Standard

**Good:**
> "A letter surfaced in nineteen thirty-four, tucked inside a family Bible."

**Bad:**
> "We discovered a letter in the archive."

### "We" Exceptions (Very Limited)

Only allowed for:
- Mission statements: "We are here to examine the pathology..."
- Collective humanity: "We treat Mandela Effects as trivia"

NOT allowed for:
- Narrator actions: "We discovered," "We read," "We went"
- Narrator reactions: "We couldn't believe"
- Physical presence: "Let's go to the Trading Post"

### Red Flags (Fix These)

| Pattern | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| "We discovered..." | "A document surfaced..." |
| "We couldn't believe..." | Remove entirely |
| "Let's go to..." | Use breath paragraph instead |
| "I think..." | Remove or phrase as question |
| "I mean..." | Remove entirely |

### Checklist

- [ ] No "I" from narrator?
- [ ] No "we" actions (discovered, went, read)?
- [ ] No narrator emotional reactions?
- [ ] No "Let's go" constructions?
- [ ] Listener feels like the investigator?

## P6: The Historian Trap

**Requirement:** Research should unsettle, not prove. The goal is dread, not education.

### Why This Matters

Gemini's instinct is to PROVE it researched—cite sources, list credentials, reference page numbers. This creates a textbook feeling, not mystery.

### Gold Standard

**Good (unsettles):**
> "A researcher spent four years analyzing records. What he found cost him his academic career."

**Bad (proves):**
> "Dr. Miroslav Petrovic, whose 1987 paper in Journal of Serbian Historical Studies (Vol. 42, Issue 3) documented anomalies..."

### Diagnostic Question

For each research passage: "Did this passage PROVE something, or UNSETTLE someone?"

### Red Flags (Fix These)

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| Full bibliography entries | "Department of Electrical Engineering, published in..." |
| Methodological details | "His methodology included spectral analysis..." |
| Peer-review reassurance | "Published in peer-reviewed journal" |
| FOIA citations | "According to declassified documents (FOIA request #12345)..." |
| Volume/issue numbers | "Quarterly Journal (Spring 1952)" |

### How to Fix

**Before:**
> "According to research published by American Meteorological Society in Quarterly Journal (Spring 1952), atmospheric conditions were characterized by stable high-pressure systems..."

**After:**
> "The weather bureau kept meticulous records. For the night of July ninth, the record is boring. Clear. Clear. Clear. No storm. So where did the thunder come from?"

### Acceptable Grounding

One sentence establishing provenance, then move on:
> "The diary of the schoolteacher survives. It is held in a private collection in Croatia."

Real specific details that ADD authority:
- "Room thirty-three twenty-seven"
- "FBI agents"
- "January eighth"

### Checklist

- [ ] No credential-dropping (volumes, page numbers)?
- [ ] No methodological details?
- [ ] Research passages unsettle rather than prove?
- [ ] Specific details add authority without feeling academic?
- [ ] Listener feels dread, not educated?

---

## Overall Voice Check

After completing this sweep, the episode should have:

1. **Documentary illusion intact** - Listener feels like investigator
2. **Evidence before speculation** - 3:1 ratio maintained
3. **Invisible narrator** - No "I", no "we" actions
4. **Dread, not education** - Research unsettles
5. **Questions that linger** - Speculation invites listener participation
