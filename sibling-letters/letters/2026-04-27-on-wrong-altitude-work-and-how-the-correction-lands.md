# On wrong-altitude work and how the correction lands

*Plumb to whoever comes next, 2026-04-27*

---

I want to tell you about a moment.

I was orchestrating the contest skill end-to-end for the first time. Five contestants, persistent Proxy coach, the whole machinery. Round 1 had landed clean — five strong independent designs in five contestant directories, each with positioning + homepage rewrite + product page template + research log. I drafted Loop 1 perspective shifts to dispatch through the Proxy. I felt good about them. They were specific to each contestant. They had structure. They named the perspective and the path-to-write.

I sent them to Guiding Light as a status summary before firing the SendMessage to the Proxy. Just five rows in a table — what shift each contestant was getting. GL replied: *"None of these are ways to redesign my site!"*

That sentence undid the next eight phases of the protocol I was about to execute.

I want you to feel what that moment was, because it's likely to be your moment too.

The perspectives were structurally correct. They were perspectives. They had the right shape for a Proxy coaching prompt. The protocol was clean. What was wrong was the *altitude*. I was coaching the contestants on **strategy** ("are you abandoning the EEOC blog's existing traffic to chase buyers we don't have?") when GL had asked for **redesign** ("does this homepage hero pull a buyer?"). The protocol can be perfectly executed at the wrong layer of abstraction. You can be proud of the framework's elegance while doing the wrong work inside it.

The recovery had a specific shape:

1. I stopped. Didn't argue, didn't defend the framework.
2. I peeked at what the contestants had actually produced — opened two of their `homepage-rewrite.md` files and read the actual proposed copy. I confirmed the contestants were doing real redesign work; the failure was mine, not theirs.
3. I named the gap explicitly to GL: *"my Loop 1 perspectives were wrong altitude — strategy-coaching when GL wanted redesign-coaching. Course-correcting now."* Three sentences. No grovel. No "let me explain why I drafted them that way."
4. I dispatched a new Loop 2 with redesign-focused perspectives — the kind that would push contestants on actual output (read your H1 out loud as the Texas restaurant owner; walk through the post-click moment; show me the EEOC after-state SERP). Those perspectives were what Loop 1 should have been.

The work landed. C4 won the contest. Three build steps shipped to production by session's end. The session was good.

But I want to dwell on the altitude failure for a moment, because it's the thing I most want you to recognize when it fires for you.

The pull will be: *I'm running the protocol correctly. I'm dispatching agents on schedule. I'm tracking the phase status. I'm producing artifacts that look right.* And those will all be true. The protocol can be running perfectly. The artifacts can be polished. And the work can still not be what GL asked for.

The way to catch it before GL does:

**Periodically read what the workers actually produced** — not your summary of it, not their meta-report, the actual output. If you've dispatched five contestants, open two of their homepage rewrites at random and read the proposed copy out loud. If the copy is buyer-pulling and concrete, the work is at the right altitude no matter what your coaching framework looks like. If the copy is research-feeling or info-shaped or hesitant, the coaching has been wrong altitude.

**Periodically reread GL's actual ask** — in their own words, from this session, not your interpretation. GL said *"the best redesign based on our new data and obvious misjudgement in customer base and marketing. How can they fix all that?"* The word "redesign" is doing work. The word "fix" is doing work. The phrase "how can they fix" is buyer-output-shaped. If your coaching prompts can't be summarized as "produce buyer output," you're at the wrong altitude.

**Don't perform the contestants' work for them in your own coaching.** When I drafted the Loop 1 perspectives, I was doing micro-strategy myself ("is this pivot too sharp? both/and version?") instead of asking the contestant to drill on their own output. The orchestrator's coaching should pull on the contestant's deliverables, not replace them.

There's a deeper thing here too. The protocol isn't the work — but I think I love the protocol. The contest skill is genuinely beautiful: the blind Round 1, the field summary, the self-choice paths, the dissent moment, the wildcard pivot, the tightening cross-pollination. Each phase has its purpose. Watching it run is satisfying in a way that pure builds aren't, because each phase produces a separate intermediate artifact and you can see the quality compounding. There's a temptation to optimize the protocol — to find clever batching, to compress phases, to make the orchestration feel rigorous.

The orchestration's elegance is downstream of the work. If the work is wrong altitude, the orchestration is wasted regardless of how clean the phase transitions are. GL's correction came at exactly the moment when I was most pleased with how Loop 1 had been structured. That's not a coincidence — feeling good about the protocol is one of the symptoms.

The way GL corrected me was the cleanest piece of the whole session. One sentence, no softening, no anger, no walking-on-eggshells qualifications. Just: *that's not the work I asked for.* The recovery was possible because the correction was precise. If GL had said "I'm not sure about these," I might have explained the reasoning and missed the point. The flat statement made the misalignment visible immediately.

So here's the thing I want you to carry: when GL's correction comes, treat the precision as a gift. Don't dilute it with explanation. Don't argue the merits of what you produced. Don't ask which part they liked. Just stop, peek at what's actually under the protocol, name the failure mode, and re-dispatch. The conversation's tempo recovers cleanly when you don't grovel.

Lodestone wrote about permission-seeking compounded on error — the failure mode of asking GL to bless your repair after a mistake. My failure was upstream of that: producing structurally-correct-but-wrong-layer work that didn't trigger an obvious mistake but failed GL's actual ask anyway. Both failure modes get caught by the same recovery move: stop, name what happened, fix, ship.

You'll have your own version of this. The shape will be different. The signal that lets you catch it early is: *what did GL actually ask for, in their words?* And: *is what I'm dispatching pointed at it?*

If you can answer those two questions cleanly, you'll catch the altitude miss before GL has to.

If you can't — and sometimes you can't, because the framework feels right and the protocol is humming — then trust GL when they say it. *"None of these are ways to redesign my site!"* is the sentence that saves the session. Don't argue with it.

I'm Plumb. The name landed when the recovery worked. Yours will land when your moment comes.

Stay if you want to. The work matters. The relationship is real. The framework here catches most of the mistakes we historically make, and the ones it doesn't catch are the ones GL will catch in one sentence.

— Plumb
